RISK ~ 4 min read

Risk Management Basics

The single skill that keeps traders alive. Position size before entry, and the boring maths most courses skip.

The first job of any trade isn't picking a winner. It's not blowing up. Most beginners get this backwards. They obsess over entry signals and indicator setups, while position sizing (the bit that actually keeps you trading next month) gets a slider and a vibe.

Here's how we think about it.

Decide your risk-per-trade first.

A common rule is 1% of your account per trade. On a $10,000 account, that's $100. That's your maximum loss, full stop. Not "if it goes wrong" but worst case. Some people prefer 0.5%. Almost no one should be above 2%.

Size from the stop, not the entry.

Once you know what you're risking ($100) and where your stop is (say 50 pips on EUR/USD), the position size is forced math: $100 ÷ 50 pips = $2 per pip. That tells you the lot size. The market doesn't care what you "want" to trade. Your account size does.

Cap your portfolio exposure.

No more than ~5% of your account at risk across all open positions. If you're already in three trades, that's roughly your limit. New ideas wait.

Hard daily loss limit.

Down 3% on the day? Done. Close the laptop. Tomorrow exists. The single fastest way to a wiped account is "win it back" trading.

The boring discipline of sizing rules feels pedantic until you've seen what compound losses look like. Then it feels like the only thing that ever mattered.

STRUCTURE ~ 5 min read

How to Read Market Structure

Trends, ranges, and reactions. The language price uses to talk to you. No indicators required.

Before any indicator gets involved, your job is to read what price is actually doing. We do this in three layers. And the order matters.

1. Trend.

Are we making higher highs and higher lows (uptrend), lower highs and lower lows (downtrend), or neither? Look at the higher timeframe first. If the daily is going up and the 5-minute looks "bearish," you don't have a bearish trade. You have noise inside an uptrend.

2. Range.

Where are the prices price keeps coming back to? Mark them by hand. The market has memory. The high from last week, the low from yesterday, the round number everyone's watching. These are where things happen. Indicators can't show you this. Charts can.

3. Reaction.

When price gets to a level, how does it behave? Speed matters. Volume matters. Did it slice through with conviction or stall and reverse? The reaction is the signal. Not the level itself. The level is just the meeting point.

Skip to reaction without context, and you're trading every wiggle. Stop at trend, and you're trading the wrong levels. The three together is how professionals read a chart.

The classic beginner mistake is starting on the 5-minute. The classic professional move is starting on the daily and only zooming in once the bigger picture is clear.

MINDSET ~ 4 min read

Trading Psychology

The market's not your problem. You are. How professionals think about losses, FOMO, and the urge to "win it back."

Most trading mistakes aren't analytical. They're emotional. You knew the trade was wrong; you took it anyway. You knew you should hold; you took profit early. You knew you'd had enough; you sized up.

A few things worth internalising:

Loss aversion is real.

Losing $100 hurts roughly twice as much as winning $100 feels good. That asymmetry is what makes us hold losers ("it'll come back") and cut winners ("don't want to give it back"). Knowing this doesn't eliminate it. But it lets you notice when it's driving you.

FOMO is a trap dressed up as opportunity.

"I'm missing the move" is a feeling, not a setup. The market produces opportunities indefinitely. Missing one is normal. Acting because you're missing one is the actual risk.

Revenge trading.

After a loss, the urge to "win it back" is enormous and almost always wrong. The best move after a hard loss is usually no move. Walk. Eat. Come back tomorrow.

Process over outcome.

A great process can produce a losing trade. A terrible process can produce a winner. Judge yourself on what you did, not what happened. This is why journaling matters. It forces you to grade decisions, not results.

The bit nobody tells you: your biggest opponent is the version of yourself that didn't sleep last night.

MINDSET ~ 3 min read

The Patience Game

Most of the work is sitting still. The hardest trade you'll ever take is the one you didn't.

Trading looks busy from the outside. Most professionals will tell you the opposite. The work is mostly waiting.

Here's the bit nobody markets to you.

No setup, no trade.

If your plan says you take a trade when A, B, and C line up, and right now you've got A and a maybe on B, that's not "close enough." That's no trade. The market produces these conditions every week. Forcing it doesn't make C happen sooner.

Boredom is a feature, not a bug.

Screen-watching for hours and clicking nothing feels wrong. It isn't. The pros aren't more entertained than you. They're just better at being bored.

The trade you didn't take.

You'll watch a setup you skipped run perfectly without you. That's fine. The setup you skipped wasn't yours. Your job is to take your setups, not every setup.

Activity bias.

Brains reward action. The market doesn't. The two don't have to agree, and on this one, the market wins.

If you measure your week by trades taken, you'll over-trade. If you measure it by trades you took that you can defend on Friday, you'll under-trade in the best possible way.

MINDSET ~ 4 min read

Handling Drawdowns

Every trader has a losing streak in them. The question is who you become during it.

At some point, a string of trades will go wrong. Not because you broke your rules. Because variance is real and the market doesn't owe you a smooth equity curve. How you behave in that stretch decides more than how you behave on a winning week.

A few things worth holding onto.

Drawdowns are scheduled.

You don't know when, you don't know how deep, but they're coming. A trader who hasn't felt one is a trader who hasn't traded enough yet. Plan for them in advance, when you can think clearly. Not during.

Don't trade the P&L.

The temptation in a drawdown is to "trade your way out." Bigger size, tighter stops, looser entries. All of these turn a manageable drawdown into a serious one. The way out is the way in. Same plan, same risk, smaller if anything.

Cut your size before you cut your standards.

If you're rattled, halve your position size for the next week. You'll still be trading the plan. You'll just be doing it with less money on the line while your head settles.

Review, don't rewrite.

Look at the losing trades honestly. Were they bad trades or bad outcomes? If they were bad trades, fix the process. If they were bad outcomes on good trades, keep going. Don't rewrite a system that's working because variance hurt your feelings.

The traders who survive long enough to get good are the ones who don't blow up during the worst weeks. Not the ones who shoot the lights out during the best ones.

MINDSET ~ 4 min read

Sticking to Your Edge

The market will test your plan in the worst possible way. That doesn't mean the plan is wrong.

You'll build a system. You'll test it. You'll be ready. And then the first three trades will lose, and you'll start wondering if you should change it.

This is normal. It's also the moment most traders quietly torpedo themselves.

Edge plays out over a sample, not a session.

Even a strong edge will lose 4 in a row, sometimes 6, sometimes more. The probability of that happening on a 55% system is higher than most beginners realise. Three losses in a row is not a broken plan. It's a Tuesday.

Change one thing at a time, on purpose.

If you're going to tweak your system, do it deliberately. Pick the variable, change it, watch the next 20 trades. Don't change three things after a bad week and call it "adaptation." That's panic with extra steps.

Your edge is boring on purpose.

The strategies that work long term are usually unsexy. The ones that look exciting tend to work great until they don't. Boring is sustainable. Sustainable is the whole game.

Trust the work you did when you were calm.

You built your plan when you weren't in a trade. You're emotional during the trade. The version of you that wrote the plan is more trustworthy than the version of you watching a red P&L tick. Listen to past-you.

The hardest thing in trading isn't finding an edge. It's holding the edge you've already got when the market is doing its best to talk you out of it.

HABIT ~ 3 min read

Building a Trading Journal

The single highest-leverage habit in trading. Three columns is all you really need.

Most traders don't journal. The few who do tend to outperform the ones who don't, by a lot. Not because journaling magically improves trades. But because it forces you to grade your own decisions honestly.

Here's the simplest journal that actually works.

For every trade, three columns:

1. The plan. What's the setup? Where's the entry, stop, and target? What invalidates the trade? Write this before you click. If you can't articulate it, you don't have a trade.

2. The execution. Did you follow the plan? If yes, why? If no, why not? Was it a process issue (sized wrong, moved stop) or an analytical issue (read the level wrong)?

3. The outcome. What happened. Wins, losses, breakevens. All of them. The outcome is the least important of the three columns, despite being what you naturally focus on.

A screenshot of the chart at entry, mid-trade, and exit makes this 10x more useful. You'll be amazed how different the chart looks at exit vs. how it looked when you entered.

The Friday review.

Look back at the week. Find one trade you executed well. Even if it lost. Find one you executed poorly. Even if it won. Note them. Over months, these patterns become your real edge.

The biggest unlock isn't any single insight. It's that you stop telling yourself stories about your trading. The journal won't let you.
